Exercise 6: singular value decomposition¶

In this exercise we are going to explore the singular value decomposition. To kick things off, let us flex our linear algebra muscles a little bit.

Let

$$ X = \sum_{k=0}^{K-1} s_k \vec u_k \vec v_k^T $$

be the singular value decomposition of $X$. Prove that the SVD of the regularized pseudoinverse of $X$ can be written as:

$$ (X^T X + N \lambda^2 \mathbf 1)^{-1} X^T = \sum_{k=0}^{K-1} \frac{s_k}{s_k^2 + N \lambda^2} \vec v_k \vec u_k^T $$

Do we need to assume $s_k \neq 0$ in the derivation? Why or why not?

Student's answer Score: 2.0 / 2.0 (Top)

Using the following identity $$ X^{T}X = (VS\cancel{ U^{T} })(\cancel{ U }SV) = VS^{2}V $$ The regularised Pseudoinverse becomes $$ \begin{equation} \begin{split} (X^{T}X + N\lambda^{2}\mathbb{1})^{-1}X^{T} &= (VS^{2}V^{T} + N\lambda^{2}\mathbb{1})^{-1}X^{T}\\ &= (VS^{2}V^{T} + VV^{T}[N\lambda\mathbb{1}]VV^{T})^{-1}X^{T}\\ &= (V[S^{2} + N\lambda \cancel{ V^{T}V }]V^{T})^{-1}X^{T}\\ &= (V^{T})^{-1}[S^{2}+N\lambda\mathbb{1}]^{-1}\cancel{ V^{-1}V }SU^{T}\\ &= V[S^{2}+N\lambda\mathbb{1}]^{-1}SU^{T}\\ &= \sum_{k=0}^{K-1} \frac{s_{k}}{s_{k}^{2}+N\lambda^{2}}\vec{v}_{k}\vec{u}_{k}^{T} \end{split} \end{equation} $$

As long as $\lambda, N > 0$ we do not need to assume $s_k \neq 0$ because the denominator in the final fraction will always be positive.

Polynomial fitting and the Vandermonde matrix¶

Let us explore polynomial fitting a little more, since it is a very instructive example for SVD and regularization. Remember that given some $x_n$ and labels $y_n$ we wanted to fit a model: $$ \arg\min_\theta \sum_{n=0}^{N-1} \Big| y_n - \sum_{k=0}^{K-1} \theta_k x_n^k \Big|^2 = \arg\min_\theta || \vec y - X \vec\theta ||^2 $$

The design matrix in above equation is called a Vandermonde matrix: $$ X_{nk} = x_n^{k} $$ and it is famously nasty if you're not careful, which is why you might have had problems fitting in the previous exercise.

Because the singular values decrease rapidly it means that most of the interesting data is in the polynomials corresponding to the first few left singualr vectors (the lowest order ones in this case). Comments:

You made a few quite correct statements pertaining to 1) and 2), although not all that would be relevant is discussed - an answer to 3) [-1] is fully missing. Here are some comments on the whole topic:

  1. The big $s_k$ still have the largest impact on the fit, in the sense of determining, in general, the shape of the curve strongest, and driving the coefficients in the right direction (e.g. the first coefficient will set the mean of all the data/fit, this is the strongest influence a coefficient can have). The overlap between the true low-order polynomial and the strongly oscillating higher-order basis functions is however small, so that, in the absence of noise, they would actually not play a big role in the fit. ad 3) Those orders with $\left| v_{K-1,k'} \right|$ largest (where $k'$ is the polynomial order), i.e., orders 19, 21, 23 will get the most noise - because they are largest for the right singular vector which corresponds to the smallest singular value ($s_{K-1}$ is smallest).

In general, the highest polynomial orders will be strongest affected by noise due to the effect of the singular values described above – however, the left and right singular vectors do not enhance this specific effect further. When considering the effect of noise on the data, we can use the formula $\vartheta_{noise} = VS^{-1}U^T\varepsilon = \sum\limits_{k=0}^{K-1} v_k\left[\dfrac{1}{s_k}\left(u_k^T\varepsilon_k\right)\right]$ to analyze how it affects each component of the fit. According to this expression, we would expect to pick up the same amount of noise with each $u_k$ if the noise is white. However, the noise is then further amplified by $1/s_k$ and projected onto $v_k$. This means that we should expect the effect of the noise to be largest for $s_{K-1}$
